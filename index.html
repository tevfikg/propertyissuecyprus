Çözümün Kilit Taşı;Mülkiyet Konusu

Mülkiyet meselesinin çözümünün, öncelikle her iki toplumun ve ilgili uluslararası kuruluşların konu hakkında kapsamlı bir şekilde bilgilendirilmesine dayandığına inanıyorum. Bu kısa özet çalışma, tam da bu ihtiyacı karşılamak amacıyla hazırlanmıştır.

Kıbrıs Sorununun Çözümü ile Mülkiyet meselesi birbirinden ayrı konular değildir. O nedenle Kıbrıs sorununun hangi başlıkları kapsadığına bakmamız ve önemli bir referandum olan 2004 yılı Annan Planı oylamasını ele almamız gereklidir. Kıbrıs sorunu ile ilgili müzakere süreçlerinde 6 temel ana başlık belirlenmiştir. Bu başlıklar, özellikle Birleşmiş Milletler (BM) gözetiminde yürütülen çözüm arayışlarında temel konuları ifade etmektedir. Buna göre;

  1. Yönetim ve Güç Paylaşımı (Governance and Power Sharing)
  2. Mülkiyet (Property)
    1974 sonrası yer değiştirmek zorunda kalan kişilerin mülkiyet hakları, iade, tazminat ya da takas yöntemleri ile çözüm yollarını içerir. Bu konuda son gelinen durumda iki bölgelilik esası benimsenmektedir.
  3. AB Konuları (EU Matters)
  4. Ekonomi (Economy)
  5. Toprak (Territory)
    İki kurucu devletin sınırları, toprak düzenlemeleri ve harita konuları bu başlıkta yer alır. "İki bölgelilik" ilkesi de toprak dağılımının hangi oranlarda hedefleneceğini öngörür.
  6. Güvenlik ve Garantiler (Security and Guarantees)

Bu karar; on üç üye ülke tarafından kabul oyu, Pakistan tarafından karşı ve Amerika Birleşik Devletleri tarafından çekimser oy kullanılarak kabul edilmiştir.

Mal Tazmin Komisyonu

AİHM kararlarında Mal Tazmin Komisyonunu etkili bir iç hukuk yolu olarak görmemektedir. Nedenlerini de AHİM verdiği kararlarda özetle şu şekilde sıralamıştır:

  • Sadece Tazminat Yetkisi: Mal Tazmin Komisyonu, adından da anlaşılabileceği gibi, sadece tazminat ödeyebilen bir komisyondu.
  • İade ve Takas Seçeneklerinin Yokluğu: AİHM, bu komisyonun yetersiz olduğuna karar vermiştir, çünkü iade ve takas seçenekleri bulunmuyordu. Mahkeme, etkili bir iç hukuk yolunun oluşturulması için iade ve takas seçeneklerinin de olması gerektiğini belirtmiştir.
  • Yeterince bağımsız bir komisyon olmaması ve Yargıç Nitelikleri: AİHM ayrıca, verdiği kararlarda komisyonun bağımsız olması gerektiğini ve yargıçlarının belirli nitelikleri taşıması gerektiğini de ifade etmiştir.


Taşınmaz Mal Komisyonu (TMK)
(Immovable Property Commission)

2005'te kurulan bu komisyonun amacı 1974 öncesi Güney'de kalan Türk malları ve Kuzey'de kalan Rum mallarıyla ilgili mülkiyet iddialarını mal tanzim komisyonundan farklı olarak tazminat, iade veya takas yoluyla çözmektir. Tarafların anlaşarak takas ve iade gibi konularda karar almasına da olanak tanıdığı için etkili bir iç hukuk yolu olarak görülmüş ve günümüzde de görülmeye de devam etmektedir.


Loizidu Davası ve Emsal Teşkil Etmesi: Mülkiyet davaları, Loizidu davasıyla yıllar öncesinden başlamıştır. Kıbrıslı Rum Titina Loizidu'nun Girne'deki mülkleriyle ilgili Avrupa İnsan Hakları Mahkemesi'nde (AİHM) açtığı davayı kazanması, bir emsal teşkil etmiş ve ardından Dimopoulos gibi başka davaların da gündeme gelmesine neden olmuştur. Rumlar öncelikle kendi mahkemelerinde davalar açmış daha sonra aldıkları karar sonrası iç hukuk yolunu tüketip AİHM'e başvuruda bulunmuşlardır. Türkiye, başlangıçta buna ilişkin AİHM kararlarını uygulamasa da, daha sonra 2002'de iktidara gelen ve Avrupa ile o dönem anlaşmalar imzalayan ve iyi ilişkiler inşa eden AK Parti hükümeti iyi niyet göstergesi olarak Loizidou'nun tazminat hakkını ödeme kararı almıştır.

Taşınmaz Mal Komisyonu'nun Kuruluşu ve Fonksiyonu: AİHM'e çok sayıda Rum başvurusu gitmesi üzerine, AİHM, Türkiye'ye "bu davalarla baş etmek için, bir komisyon kurun: Taşınmaz Mal Komisyonu (TMK)" önerisinde bulunmuştur. İki yabancı üyenin de bulunduğu TMK kurulmuş ve bir süre sonra bu komisyona başvuran Rum vatandaşlara ödeme yapmaya başlamıştır.

TMK'nın Kuruluş Temelleri: AİHM kararlarına bağlılık, Kıbrıs Türk halkının çözüm iradesi (2004 Annan Planı referandumu ile gösterilen), ve iki bölgelilik ilkesi olmak üzere Taşınmaz Mal Komisyonu üç temel ayak üzerine inşa edilmiştir. Ve AİHM'nin kararlarında da etkili bir iç hukuk yolu olarak tanımlanmıştır.

İki Bölgelilik Esası ile şuan yürürlükte olan Taşınmaz Mal Komisyonu arasındaki bağlantıyı açıklayacak olursak

2004 yılında Kıbrıs Türk toplumunun evet dediği Annan Planında geçen ve daha sonra 2005 yılında kurulan Taşınmaz Mal Komisyonunda da temel ilke olarak benimsenmiş olan İki bölgelilik ilkesi nedir? Bu ilke Kuzey Kıbrıs sınırları içindeki mülkiyetin en az üçte ikisinin Kıbrıs Türk mülkiyetinde olması gerektiğini öngören ilkedir. Bu ilkenin amaçları; kuzey'deki Türk kurucu devletinin sınırları içinde kalan mülkiyetin en az üçte ikisinin (yani %66,7'sinin) Kıbrıslı Türklerin mülkiyetinde kalacak şekilde düzenlemelerin yapılması ve böylece Türk kurucu devletinde nüfusun da ağırlıklı olarak (büyük çoğunlukla) Kıbrıslı Türklerden oluşmasıdır. Bu ilke ile iki bölgeli yapının demografik ve ekonomik temelinin korunmasını amaçlamıştır, Türk bölgesinde Türklerin mülkiyet ve nüfus çoğunluğunun muhafaza edilmesi iki bölgelilik ilkesi ile hedeflenmiştir.

Loizidou v. Turkey (1996)

Loizidou, 1974'ten beri KKTC'deki taşınmazlarına erişimin engellendiğini ve mülkiyet hakkının ihlal edildiğini ileri sürdü. Karar: AİHM, Türkiye'nin 1 No'lu Protokol'ün 1. maddesini (mülkiyet hakkı) ve Sözleşme'nin 8. maddesini (özel yaşam ve konut) ihlal ettiğine hükmetti. "The Court considers that the denial of access to the applicant's property and the ensuing loss of control constitutes an interference with her rights guaranteed under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1." (Mahkeme, başvurucunun mülküne erişiminin engellenmesini ve kontrol kaybını 1 No'lu Protokol madde 1'de güvence altına alınan hakkına bir müdahale olarak değerlendirmektedir.)

Orams v. Apostolides


(2009, İngiltere mahkemeleri / AB Adalet Divanı ilişkili emsal karar)

Güney Kıbrıs mahkemesinin Orams çifti hakkında verdiği mülkiyetten tahliye kararının İngiltere'de icrası istendi. O yıl İngiltere AB üyesiydi. Orams çifti (David Charles Orams ve Linda Elizabeth Orams) İngiliz vatandaşıydı. Karar : İngiltere mahkemeleri ve AB Adalet Divanı, Rum mahkemesi kararının AB içinde icra edilebileceğine karar verdi. Bu kararın ortaya çıkmasındaki tüm hukuki süreci sırası ile inceleyecek olursak: Davacı : Meletios Apostolides (Kıbrıslı Rum, Güney Kıbrıs vatandaşı) Davalılar : David Charles Orams ve Linda Elizabeth Orams (İngiliz vatandaşı bir çift) Talep Konusu : 1974 öncesinde Apostolides'e ait olan KKTC sınırlarındaki taşınmaz üzerine Orams çifti mülk edindi (satın aldı) ve villa inşa etti. Apostolides, bu mülkün kendisine iadesini ve yapılan yapıların yıkılmasını talep etti. Güney Kıbrıs Rum Mahkemesi, Orams çiftinin mülkü boşaltmasına ve villa ile diğer yapıların yıkılmasına karar verdi. Apostolides bu kararı İngiltere'de icraya koymak istedi.Ve böylece İngiltere'de yargı süreci başladı. İngiltere Yüksek Mahkemesi başlangıçta Rum mahkemesi kararını icra etmeyi reddetti çünkü mülkün KKTC'de bulunduğunu ve fiili imkansızlık sebebiyle fiilen icra edilemeyeceğini belirtti.Apostolides vazgeçmeyerek temyize gitti; ve konu AB hukukunun uygulanması açısından AB Adalet Divanı'na taşındı. Adalet Divanı ise kararını verdi.Bu karar:

AB Adalet Divanı (ECJ) Kararı (28 Nisan 2009)

"AB içinde bir üye devlet mahkemesinin verdiği karar, diğer üye devletler tarafından tanınmak ve icra edilmek zorundadır. Mülkün bulunduğu yerde kararın fiilen uygulanamaması (ör. villa KKTC'de olduğu için yıkılamaması), İngiltere'de mal varlığına el konulmasına ve icranın başka yollarla yapılmasına engel değildir. Şeklinde bir karar çıktı.

"The suspension of the acquis communautaire in the northern area of Cyprus does not preclude the recognition and enforcement in another Member State of a judgment given by a court of the Republic of Cyprus." (AB müktesebatının Kuzey Kıbrıs'ta askıya alınmış olması, Rum Cumhuriyeti mahkemesinin kararının başka bir Üye Devlette tanınmasına ve icrasına engel değildir.) AB Adalet Divanı kararına dayanarak İngiltere mahkemesi ise Rum mahkemesi kararının İngiltere'de icrasına hükmetti. Böylece Orams çiftinin İngiltere'deki mallarına el konulması ve tazminat ödemesi yoluyla kararın icrası mümkün oldu. "Judgments given in default of appearance in civil and commercial matters are to be recognised and enforced in other Member States regardless of the political situation of the area where the property is located." (Sivil ve ticari konularda gıyapta verilen kararlar, taşınmazın bulunduğu bölgedeki siyasi durumdan bağımsız olarak diğer Üye Devletlerde tanınacak ve icra edilecektir.)

Peki mevcut durumda Güneydeki yönetimin kendi bölgelerinde kalan Kıbrıslı Türklerin bıraktığı taşınmazlara ilişkin politikası nedir?

1974'te yaşanan olayların ardından adanın güneyinde kalan Kıbrıslı Türklere ait taşınmazların hukuki statüsü ve yönetimi, Kıbrıs Cumhuriyeti tarafından özel bir yasal düzenlemeyle korunmaktadır. "Türk Mallarını Koruma Yasası" (The Law for the Administration of Turkish Cypriot Properties) olarak bilinen ilgili mevzuat çerçevesinde, söz konusu mülkler "Türk Malı" (Turkish-Cypriot owned property) olarak kaydedilmiş ve devletin koruması altına alınmıştır. Bu malların idaresi, Kıbrıs Cumhuriyeti İçişleri Bakanlığı'na bağlı olarak faaliyet gösteren Türk Mallarını Koruma Dairesi (Guardian of Turkish Cypriot Properties) tarafından yürütülmektedir.

(https://www.olc.gov.cy/olc/olc.nsf/D3F889B23F16B2DDC22587A3002D762C/$file/Turkish-Cypriot.pdf)

Bu politikanın temel amacı ve ilkesi, mülklerin asıl sahipleri olan Kıbrıslı Türkler adına korunması ve onların yokluğunda satılmasının veya mülkiyetinin kalıcı olarak devredilmesinin önlenmesidir. Buna göre, söz konusu taşınmazlar öncelikli olarak 1974'te kuzeyden güneye göç etmek zorunda kalmış Kıbrıslı Rumlara hukuki kapsam olarak geçici-kiralama yöntemi ile kiralanmaktadır. Ancak bu tahsisin, kiracıya mülkiyet hakkı kazandırmayacağı Türk Mallarını Koruma Yasasında özel olarak düzenlenmiştir. Örneğin, şuan adanın Güneyinde yer alan Larnaka'da bir Kıbrıslı Türk'e ait evi kiralayan bir Rum, bu evi satamaz, devredemez veya çocuklarına miras olarak bırakamaz. Bu taşınmazlarla ilgili devam eden kira sözleşmeleri hukuki kapsam olarak, mülkün asıl sahibinin hakkını ortadan kaldırmayan geçici birer tedbir niteliğindedir. Bu uygulamanın altında yatan felsefe, ilerde bir tarihte adada kapsamlı bir siyasi çözüme ulaşılması durumunda Kıbrıslı Türk maliklerin geri dönmesi halinde mülklerinin kendilerine sorunsuz şekilde iade edileceği prensibine dayanır. Kıbrıs Cumhuriyeti, bu yönetim modelini uluslararası platformlarda, özellikle de Avrupa İnsan Hakları Mahkemesi nezdinde, mülkiyet hakkına saygının bir gereği olarak bugüne kadar savunmuştur.

The "Compensation Commission" and the "Immovable Property Commission" are often confused. It is important to emphasize that these two commissions are quite different in many respects.

Compensation Commission

The ECHR does not consider the Compensation Commission an effective domestic remedy. The reasons are summarized in the ECHR's decisions as follows:

  • Compensation Only: As the name suggests, the Compensation Commission could only pay compensation.
  • Lack of Return and Exchange Options: The ECHR found this commission inadequate because there were no options for return or exchange. The court stated that an effective domestic remedy must also include return and exchange options.
  • Lack of Sufficient Independence and Judicial Qualifications: The ECHR also stated that the commission must be independent and its judges must have certain qualifications.


Immovable Property Commission (IPC)

The purpose of this commission, established in 2005, is to resolve property claims regarding Turkish properties left in the South and Greek properties left in the North before 1974, through compensation, return, or exchange, unlike the compensation commission. Since it allows parties to agree on matters such as exchange and return, it has been considered and continues to be considered an effective domestic remedy by the ECHR.


The Loizidou Case and Its Precedent: Property cases began years ago with the Loizidou case. The victory of Greek Cypriot Titina Loizidou in her case at the ECHR regarding her properties in Kyrenia set a precedent and led to other cases such as Dimopoulos. Greek Cypriots first filed lawsuits in their own courts and, after exhausting domestic remedies, applied to the ECHR. Although Turkey initially did not implement the ECHR decisions, the AK Party government, which came to power in 2002 and signed agreements with Europe, decided to pay Loizidou's compensation as a goodwill gesture.

Establishment and Function of the IPC: After numerous Greek Cypriot applications to the ECHR, the court suggested to Turkey to establish a commission to handle these cases: the Immovable Property Commission (IPC). The IPC, which includes two foreign members, was established and soon began making payments to Greek Cypriot applicants.


Ongoing Activities and Past Statistics of the IPC

Data on the IPC are continuously updated and published on the commission's website. (https://tamk.gov.ct.tr/tr-tr/)

According to the data on the website, from its establishment to the present (June 2025), a total of 8,178 applications have been made, and 1,778 of these have been concluded. A total of £538,050,407 (five hundred thirty-eight million pounds) has been paid to rights holders to date. This figure is constantly updated on the website. Payments in recent years have increased significantly. The high fees and taxes (about 12%) collected during the transfer of equivalent immovables in the TRNC contribute to the compensation expenditures of the IPC.


Foundations of the IPC: Commitment to ECHR decisions, the solution will of the Turkish Cypriot people (as shown in the 2004 Annan Plan referendum), and the principle of bi-zonality are the three main pillars on which the IPC is based. The ECHR has also defined it as an effective domestic remedy in its decisions.

If we explain the connection between the principle of bi-zonality and the current IPC

The principle of bi-zonality, which was included in the Annan Plan approved by the Turkish Cypriot community in 2004 and later adopted as a fundamental principle in the IPC established in 2005, is as follows: This principle stipulates that at least two-thirds of the property within the borders of Northern Cyprus must remain in Turkish Cypriot ownership. The aim is to ensure that at least two-thirds (66.7%) of the property within the borders of the Turkish constituent state in the North remains in the hands of Turkish Cypriots, thereby maintaining the demographic and economic basis of the bi-zonal structure. The principle aims to preserve the majority of Turkish Cypriot ownership and population in the Turkish region.


Some Important ECHR Decisions on the Property Issue

Loizidou v. Turkey (1996)

Loizidou claimed that she had been prevented from accessing her immovable properties in the TRNC since 1974 and that her property rights had been violated. Decision: The ECHR ruled that Turkey had violated Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (property rights) and Article 8 (private life and home) of the Convention. "The Court considers that the denial of access to the applicant's property and the ensuing loss of control constitutes an interference with her rights guaranteed under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1."

In the Loizidou case, which was the first of its kind:


Application: 22 July 1989 Admissibility decision: 5 March 1990 Interim decision on jurisdiction: 23 March 1995 Decision on the merits: 18 December 1996 Turkey paid the compensation to Loizidou in 2003.


Xenides-Arestis v. Turkey (2005)

The applicant claimed that her right to access her property in Famagusta was violated. Decision: The ECHR found that Turkey had violated the right to property and home; an effective domestic remedy needed to be established. "The Court finds that the continuous denial of access to the applicant's property and loss of control thereof amounts to a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 and Article 8 of the Convention."

Orams v. Apostolides


(2009, UK courts / ECJ related precedent)

The enforcement of a South Cyprus court's eviction order against the Orams couple was sought in the UK. At the time, the UK was an EU member. The Orams couple (David Charles Orams and Linda Elizabeth Orams) were British citizens. Decision: The UK courts and the ECJ ruled that the Greek Cypriot court's decision could be enforced within the EU. The legal process leading to this decision is as follows: Plaintiff: Meletios Apostolides (Greek Cypriot, South Cyprus citizen) Defendants: David Charles Orams and Linda Elizabeth Orams (British couple) Subject: The Orams couple acquired property and built a villa on land in the TRNC that belonged to Apostolides before 1974. Apostolides demanded the return of the property and the demolition of the buildings. The South Cyprus court ordered the Orams couple to vacate the property and demolish the villa and other structures. Apostolides sought to enforce this decision in the UK, and thus the legal process began in the UK. The UK High Court initially refused to enforce the Greek Cypriot court's decision, citing practical impossibility since the property was in the TRNC. Apostolides appealed, and the matter was referred to the ECJ for the application of EU law. The ECJ ruled:

ECJ Decision (28 April 2009)

"The suspension of the acquis communautaire in the northern area of Cyprus does not preclude the recognition and enforcement in another Member State of a judgment given by a court of the Republic of Cyprus."

Based on the ECJ decision, the UK court ruled to enforce the Greek Cypriot court's decision in the UK. Thus, the Orams couple's assets in the UK could be seized and the decision enforced through other means. "Judgments given in default of appearance in civil and commercial matters are to be recognised and enforced in other Member States regardless of the political situation of the area where the property is located."

Hadjithomas and Others v. Turkey (Hurma case, 2007)

The applicants claimed that they were prevented from accessing and that their properties in Hurma village near Nicosia were expropriated. Decision: The ECHR found that Turkey had violated the right to property. "The applicants have been denied access to, and control, use and enjoyment of their properties. This amounts to a violation of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1."

Demopoulos and Others v. Turkey (2010)

In this decision, the ECHR referred to the Annan Plan for the first time. The court considered the Annan Plan as one of the comprehensive solution proposals previously reached between the parties for resolving property issues in Cyprus. The ECHR stated that the solutions proposed in the Annan Plan, especially the "return, compensation, or exchange" system for property issues, were similar to internationally accepted solutions. In this context, the ECHR considered the IPC in the TRNC, which operates based on these principles, as an effective domestic remedy. This was a positive development for Turkey, as the ECHR recognized the domestic legal mechanism in the TRNC for the first time.

What is the policy of the Southern administration regarding Turkish Cypriot properties left in their region?

After the events of 1974, the legal status and management of Turkish Cypriot properties left in the South are protected by special legislation enacted by the Republic of Cyprus. Under the relevant legislation known as the "Law for the Administration of Turkish Cypriot Properties," these properties are registered as "Turkish-Cypriot owned property" and placed under state protection. The administration of these properties is carried out by the Guardian of Turkish Cypriot Properties, operating under the Ministry of the Interior of the Republic of Cyprus.

(https://www.olc.gov.cy/olc/olc.nsf/D3F889B23F16B2DDC22587A3002D762C/$file/Turkish-Cypriot.pdf)

The main purpose and principle of this policy is to protect the properties on behalf of their original Turkish Cypriot owners and to prevent their sale or permanent transfer in their absence. Accordingly, these properties are primarily leased to Greek Cypriots who migrated from the North to the South in 1974, under a temporary leasing system. However, this allocation does not grant ownership rights to the lessee, as specifically regulated in the Law for the Administration of Turkish Cypriot Properties. For example, a Greek Cypriot renting a house belonging to a Turkish Cypriot in Larnaca in the South cannot sell, transfer, or bequeath this house. The ongoing lease agreements for these properties are temporary measures that do not eliminate the original owner's rights. The underlying philosophy is that, in the event of a comprehensive political solution on the island in the future, Turkish Cypriot owners will be able to reclaim their properties without any problems. The Republic of Cyprus has defended this management model in international forums, especially before the ECHR, as a requirement of respect for property rights.

Penal Code Cap.154 and 1995 Amendment

Article 280A added by the 1995 Amendment: Acts such as "unauthorized use, possession, leasing, occupation" of immovable property in the North were made criminal offenses. Penalty: Imprisonment and/or fine.

The 1995 amendment cannot be applied to acts before this date.

As also adopted in the ECHR and international law, criminal laws cannot be applied retroactively to the detriment of the accused.

This is regulated in Article 7 of the European Convention on Human Rights and Article 12(2) of the Constitution of the Republic of Cyprus (RoC).


Constitution of the Republic of Cyprus, Article 12(2)

"No one shall be punished for an act which was not a criminal offense at the time it was committed. No heavier penalty shall be imposed subsequently."

Criminal liability arises only for acts after 1995. No criminal liability arises for acts before 1995 (Non-retroactivity Principle)

Recent Arrests and Red Notice Decisions

The Arrest of Simon Aykut

Simon Aykut is an Israeli businessman with real estate investments in the TRNC. On 7 June 2024, he arrived at Larnaca Airport in the South for a business trip and was detained by Greek Cypriot police while about to fly to Israel. There were numerous complaints against him regarding the illegal use and irregular sale of former Greek properties in the TRNC. After being detained, he was interrogated by Nicosia Greek police and then arrested. Thus, a difficult trial process began for Simon Aykut. In the days following his detention, the court decided to continue his detention due to the seriousness of the charges and the risk of tampering with evidence. Since 7 June 2024, Aykut's detention has been continuously extended at each hearing, and he has been held in prison for over a year. His lawyers argued in court that Aykut needed treatment for suspected cancer and that his illness had worsened due to poor prison conditions, making his continued detention inappropriate for his health.


Hearings were frequently postponed, new witnesses were added to the case, and the scope of the case was expanded by the court. At the hearing on 28 May 2025, recordings of an interview Aykut gave to a Turkish TV channel in 2023 were also presented as evidence. Criticisms continue that the trial is being conducted under the influence of politics and public opinion. The continued use of the most severe measure, long-term detention, and the repeated postponement of the trial suggest that the Greek Cypriot judiciary is using the law as a tool of politics in this case. In addition, the court ordered the seizure of Aykut's real estate investments in South Cyprus.


On Sunday, 7 July 2024, German real estate agent Ewa Isabella Künzel (49) was detained by Greek Cypriot police at Larnaca Airport in the South while about to fly to Germany. The next day, 8 July 2024, Isabella was arrested and, like Simon Aykut, remains in custody, with her trial ongoing and repeatedly postponed. A Hungarian real estate agent was also arrested and is being tried in the same way.


In investigations into the property issue in South Cyprus to date, a strategy has been observed in which a certain number of people from each profession and status have been prosecuted and arrested. These groups include construction company officials, real estate agents, architects, lawyers, and other intermediaries. Arrest warrants are regularly issued for people from all walks of life, and new lists are published regularly. Thus, the Greek Cypriot administration uses the law and judiciary as a tool in its policy and aims to prevent further real estate and construction activities on these properties. Since it is not possible to issue arrest and red notice decisions for tens of thousands of people, this situation has been diversified according to professions and positions.


The continued use of the most severe measure, long-term detention, as a political tool to instill fear, in violation of the European Convention on Human Rights and human rights, has been observed especially in the past year. The fact that Simon Aykut's properties in both South Cyprus and Thessaloniki, Greece, are subject to precautionary measures due to the ongoing trial, and that he has been detained for over a year, clearly demonstrates the disproportionate use of detention as a measure.


Criticisms and Debates Regarding the Turkish Administration

It is a serious finding and past criticism that the lands distributed for agricultural purposes after 1974 were acquired very cheaply, later converted into plots and sold at much higher prices, and the state did not generate enough revenue from these changes. The state should have created and accumulated a budget from the capital gains obtained after these distributions and supported a future commission to be established in a possible solution. This situation undoubtedly played a significant role in the current property problems in the North. Those who came from the South due to the conflicts before and during 1974 were given land in the North in exchange for the land they left in the South. Another major criticism is that tens of thousands of Turkish Cypriots who did not migrate (i.e., who did not leave their property in the South and move to the North) and were already living in the North were also given land ownership free of charge, and not only that, but they were also allowed to sell their land. Another criticism of past policies is that those who came from the South were given much larger plots as equivalents than the size or approximate value of the property they left behind, without proper calculation. The lack of a standard and fairness in these transfers is another criticism. Later, these properties were also sold to foreign nationals, and some of the ownership changed hands over time. Especially after 2004 (with the predictability brought by the Annan Plan), construction on these properties accelerated, and in 2020, especially in the Iskele (Trikomo) region, the remaining lands were opened for development, and thousands of residences and living spaces were built. Later, after the start of the Russia-Ukraine war, thousands of apartments were sold to foreigners, most of whom are not Cypriot citizens. Today, the TRNC collects a tax of 12% of the sale value from the sale of equivalent properties, and the revenue thus obtained contributes to the compensation payments to be made by the IPC.


Current Types of Title Deeds and Their Legal Status in the TRNC

In the TRNC today, 1974 is the milestone for existing title deeds. Accordingly, properties that did not belong to Greek Cypriots before 1974 are called "Turkish Title Deeds" and are considered properties with no ownership issues. Other immovable properties left by Greek Cypriots in the North in 1974 and allocated to Turkish Cypriots are basically divided into two: "equivalent" and "allocated." However, there is no legal or criminal difference between Equivalent Title Deeds and Allocated Title Deeds under the laws of the Republic of Cyprus (RoC). The laws of the Republic of Cyprus define the title deeds issued by the TRNC as fake and invalid. Therefore, there is no difference between these two types of title deeds in terms of legal and criminal consequences under the laws of the Republic of Cyprus.

In Conclusion

The property issue in Cyprus is directly linked to the island's historical and political division and remains one of the most difficult issues to resolve. The status of immovable properties in the North, claims regarding Greek and Turkish property rights, ECHR decisions, and international legal norms all have both legal and political dimensions. Although the Immovable Property Commission (IPC) established to date has been recognized as an effective domestic remedy by the ECHR, it faces some criticisms due to delays and financial difficulties. The legal and criminal regulations of the Greek Cypriot Administration of Southern Cyprus regarding immovable properties in the North, especially with the criminalization of such matters after 1995 and subsequent amendments in 2006, have added new tensions to the property issue. These later amendments also criminalized not only the acquisition but also the leasing, construction, and brokerage of such properties.


The property issue is not only about the individual claims of property owners but also constitutes one of the main building blocks of lasting peace and a solution on the island. Increasing the effectiveness of the IPC, ensuring that the process is conducted transparently, quickly, and impartially, and building trust in accordance with international legal norms are essential, but it cannot be expected that the IPC alone will largely solve the property issue.


The solution to the property issue can only be achieved not only by addressing Greek properties in the North but also by protecting the rights of Turkish properties left in the South and developing concrete mechanisms such as establishing research and monitoring commissions. No serious research or monitoring commission has yet been established in the TRNC regarding Turkish properties left in the South. In addition, opportunities for dialogue and trilateral meetings under the United Nations must be effectively utilized, and it should be explained in detail that the escalating tension in the property issue is harming the peace process aimed at on the island.
